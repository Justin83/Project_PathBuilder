Characteristics of Life

What do all living things have in common — and how can we tell whether something is alive?

← Back to course home

Big Question

What makes something alive?

Learning Goals

By the end of this week, you should be able to:

  • List and define the eight characteristics shared by all living things.
  • Give a real-world example for each characteristic.
  • Classify objects or organisms as living, nonliving, or once-living and explain your reasoning using evidence.
  • Use specific vocabulary and observations — not just a "feeling" — to support your classification decisions.

Key Ideas

All living things share a set of characteristics that together define what it means to be alive. No single characteristic is enough on its own — a rock can grow by accumulating minerals, but that does not make it alive.

Characteristic What It Means Example
Made of Cells All living things are made of one or more cells — the basic unit of life. A bacterium is one cell; a human is trillions of cells.
Metabolism Living things use energy to carry out life processes (breaking down food, building molecules, etc.). Your body uses the food you eat for energy and growth.
Growth and Development Living things grow and change in predictable, organized ways. A seed grows into a specific type of plant, not a random one.
Response to Environment Living things detect and respond to stimuli in their environment. A plant bends toward light; you pull your hand away from something hot.
Reproduction Living things can produce new offspring, passing hereditary information forward. A cat produces kittens; a bacterium divides into two bacteria.
Homeostasis Living things regulate their internal conditions to stay stable. Your body keeps its temperature near 98.6°F even when it's cold outside.
Heredity (DNA) Living things carry and pass on genetic information using DNA. You inherited traits from your biological parents through DNA.
Adaptation Populations of living things develop traits over generations that help them survive in their environment. Polar bears have thick fur adapted to cold climates.

Assignment Directions

Part 1: Characteristics of Life Notes

In your science notebook, create a reference page for the 8 characteristics of life. For each characteristic:

  1. Write the name of the characteristic.
  2. Write your own definition in 1–2 sentences.
  3. Give one real-world example that is different from the example in the table above.

Part 2: Living / Nonliving / Once-Living Sorting Worksheet

You will receive a sorting worksheet (in your printed packet or from your parent) with a list of items to classify. For each item:

  1. Write whether it is living, nonliving, or once-living.
  2. Choose at least three items and write 2–3 sentences explaining why using specific characteristics of life as evidence.
Evidence Requirement: "It's a rock so it's nonliving" is not evidence — it's a conclusion. Your explanation must name one or more characteristics of life and explain why the item does or does not show that characteristic. For example: "A dried leaf is once-living because it was made of cells and carried out metabolism, but it is no longer doing either."
